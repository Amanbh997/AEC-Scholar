Prepare the user for their viva / thesis defense: **$ARGUMENTS**

Use the **research-strategist** and **peer-reviewer** agents and the `thesis-dissertation` skill.

1. **Contribution statement.** Help the user articulate a crisp 3–5 minute summary of the thesis's original
   contribution and how the studies connect (the "golden thread").

2. **Anticipated questions.** Generate a realistic question bank across the categories examiners use:
   - *Contribution & originality* — "What is your original contribution? Why does it matter?"
   - *Method & rigor* — "Why these methods? What are the alternatives you rejected and why?"
   - *Validity & limitations* — "What are the threats to validity? How far do your findings **generalize**?"
     (AEC examiners press hard on generalizability — single case/building/simulation.)
   - *Integration* — "How do your chapters/papers fit together?"
   - *Positioning* — "How does this differ from [key prior work]?"
   - *Reflexivity* — "What would you do differently? What's next?"
   - *Practical relevance* — "What does industry do with this?"
   For each, draft a strong, honest model answer and the key points to hit.

3. **Know the weak points first.** Surface the thesis's most likely lines of attack (unvalidated assumptions,
   sample/scale, over-claims) and prepare measured, non-defensive responses + an errata list.

4. **Rehearsal.** Offer to run a **mock viva** — ask questions one at a time, let the user answer, and give
   constructive feedback on substance and framing.

Tone: build genuine confidence through preparation, not bravado. Encourage honest acknowledgment of
limitations over defensiveness.
