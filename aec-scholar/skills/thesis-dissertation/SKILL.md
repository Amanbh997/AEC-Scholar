# Thesis & Dissertation — Structure Guide

Help researchers design and write a coherent thesis. The unifying requirement is a clear, defensible
**central contribution** that the whole document builds and demonstrates.

## 1. Choose the format (and check institutional rules)
- **Monograph** — one continuous book-length argument (traditional; common in some EU/UK contexts).
- **Compilation / publication-based / "stapled"** — a set of published/submitted papers framed by an
  overarching synthesis (the **kappa** in Nordic systems / **introductory chapter**). Common in engineering
  and increasingly the norm in AEC. Each paper is a chapter; the synthesis makes them one contribution.
- **Hybrid** — some chapters are papers, others are bespoke.
> Format, length, paper-count and co-authorship rules are **institution-specific** — tell the user to confirm
> their university's regulations.

## 2. Monograph architecture (typical)
1. **Introduction** — problem, significance, **gap**, aim, research questions, contribution, thesis roadmap.
2. **Literature review / background** — synthesize the field, define the framework, justify the gap.
3. **Methodology** — overarching research design and philosophy (link `research-methods`).
4–N. **Results/analysis chapters** — usually one per study/RQ.
N+1. **Discussion** — integrate findings, compare with literature, implications, limitations.
N+2. **Conclusion** — restate the contribution, answer the RQs, future work.
+ References, appendices.

## 3. Compilation / three-paper model
- A common shape: **3–5 papers** forming a logical arc (e.g., *review → framework/method → empirical
  validation → application/field study*). Each builds on the last.
- The **synthesis ("kappa"/introductory) chapter** is the real thesis: it states the overarching aim &
  RQs, positions all papers in the literature, explains how each paper answers which RQ, synthesizes the
  **combined** contribution (more than the sum of papers), and discusses integrated implications,
  limitations and future work. Examiners weight this heavily.
- Address **authorship** transparently (your contribution to co-authored papers — use CRediT; see
  `research-ethics-integrity`) and **copyright/permissions** for republished papers.

## 4. The golden thread (coherence)
- A single, explicit research aim and a small set of RQs that **every** chapter maps to.
- Consistent terminology, framework and narrative across chapters; bridging text so papers don't read as
  disconnected silos.
- A contribution statement that is identical in spirit in the introduction, synthesis and conclusion.

## 5. Planning & milestones
- Back-plan from submission: literature → proposal/confirmation → studies (sequenced) → writing → internal
  review → submission → viva → corrections. Build slack for fieldwork/data-access delays (chronic in AEC).
- Track per-paper status (planned/data/analysis/draft/under review/published) and which RQ each serves.
  (See `/aec-scholar:research-plan` for a Gantt.)

## 6. Writing strategy
- Write continuously (don't leave all writing to the end); turn each study into a paper as you go (in the
  compilation model the papers *are* the chapters).
- Apply `academic-writing` per chapter; keep an evolving "contribution" paragraph you refine throughout.
- Manage references in one library from day one (`citation-formats`); use a thesis LaTeX class or styled
  template (`latex-academic`) with `\include` per chapter and a glossary for acronyms.

## 7. Viva / defense preparation
- Re-read the whole thesis; prepare a crisp 3–5 minute summary of the contribution.
- Anticipate examiner questions: *What is your original contribution? Why these methods? What are the
  limitations and threats to validity? How do your studies connect? What would you do differently? How does
  this generalize?* (AEC examiners probe generalizability hard.) Prepare honest, confident answers.
- Know your weak points before they do; have a list of errata and a measured stance on each limitation.
- Practice with mock vivas. (See `/aec-scholar:viva`.)

## 8. Common pitfalls
- A compilation with no real synthesis chapter (papers stapled together ≠ a thesis).
- RQs that drift between chapters; inconsistent framing/terminology.
- Over-claiming a unified contribution the studies don't jointly support.
- Ignoring institutional format/length/authorship rules.
- Leaving the synthesis and writing too late.
